ABOUT THE STUDIO

Games that shouldn't work... but somehow do.

Who We Are

Solo game developer. Flow Programmer.

I don't code the traditional way. I direct, refine, and obsessively iterate with AI tools until the game in my head becomes the one on the screen. Pixel art RPGs, interactive stories, and whatever else refuses to leave me alone. One person. No team. No shortcuts on the feel.

Why "Brok3n by Design"?

Look, "Flawless by Design" felt like a lie, and the domain was probably taken anyway.

The honest version: everything here starts as a glorious mess — half-finished systems, features held together with tape and stubbornness, and at least one bug I've officially reclassified as "a mechanic." Then I iterate until the broken parts either become the best part or quietly vanish before anyone notices. The cracks aren't a flaw in the plan. They are the plan.

And the "3" instead of an "e"? Every indie dev is contractually required to swap at least one vowel for a number. I don't make the rules. I just quietly resent them.

What We Believe

No Shortcuts on the Feel

A game can be rough around the edges, but it has to feel right. I'll rebuild the same system ten times until it plays the way it does in my head.

Flow Programming

I don't write traditional code — I direct and wrestle AI tools the way a designer wrestles systems, iterating hard and cutting whatever doesn't earn its place.

Strange Beats Safe

I'd rather make something memorable and a little broken than something polished and forgettable.

One Person, All In

No team, no committee, no "good enough." Every pixel, sound, and awkward joke is a deliberate choice by one stubborn human.

The One-Person Studio

One-person game studio. Part-time. Full obsession.

I'm a lifelong gamer and compulsive idea machine who finally got tired of waiting for a team I can't afford. I don't write traditional code. I direct, refine, and wrestle with AI tools the same way a game designer wrestles with systems — iterating hard, cutting what doesn't feel right, and rebuilding until the thing actually plays the way it does in my head.

Most of the work is the part people never see: endless fine-tuning, visual polish in Photoshop, video editing, balancing, and the quiet perfectionism that keeps me up at night. I call the process Flow Programming — staying in the current of an idea and shaping it through constant back-and-forth until it stops feeling like a prototype and starts feeling like a game.

Everything here is made by one stubborn Canadian with too many ideas and zero patience for "good enough."
